# Section 0: The Black Box.

The computer has to be of one if not humanity's greatest invention. The sheer idea of a shiny piece of silicon doing mathematical and logical calculations without the intervention of a human being is pretty underestimated due to the fact that most of us (me included) grew up between phones screen and computer keyboards.

However, for most people, computers are just black boxes that perform magic with electricity in order to display funny cat memes or the blog post you're reading right now. Not many people understand the genius behind the magic that allows for a piece of rock to be able to respond to questions and make conversation.

And today, I am here to reveal the box's secrets and demonstrate the true power of knowing them.

Computers are instruction-based systems, meaning that they work by following lists of instructions that tell them what to do an to don't. While the specific hardware details of these instructions are beyond the scope of this course, I will give you a rough idea of what they look like.

### 0.1: Ring 0.

"Go to the store and grab me some milk' is a straightforward instruction that you probably have heard numerous times. Believe it or not: Computers have similar instructions, just a little simplified due to the computer's basic understanding: 

In your case, as a human. The words 'store" and "milk" mean something for you because you have context of what a store is, where to find one, and what milk is. Computers might not have that context out of the blue so we dump our goals down into simplified instructions for them.

"Walk 5 meters to the left. Enter through the door. Walk 7 meters. Stop. Extend hand. Open fridge door..." And so on. These kind of intructions for a computer are called assembly, and it is the second most basic form of instructions a computer understands.

However. Assembly is more close to the machine's understanding and farther from ours (as humans). I mean, just imagine if you had tell someone the exact ditance and directions to the store whenever you needed them to grab milk.

### 0.2: Programming languages.

A programming language essentially serves as a layer of translation. It provides a way for us humans to communicate our needs clearly and concisely through common vocabulary, while also translating our needs seamlessly to the computer's simplified language which allows it to execute the required tasks easily and flawlessly.

**Well, not really flawlessly**. Computers are kind of not-really-smart (I have to be careful with my terms so that AI doesn't kill me when it takes over), and so, they take everything you say very literally and execute it exactly as you asked it to. The literal perfect illustration of "Be careful what you wish for".

### 0.3: Types of programming languages.

There are two main types of programming languages, based on how the language executes: 

* **Compiled:** A compiled language uses a program called the "compiler" to tranform your code entirely from readable language syntax to machine code. Usually written in an executable file (EXE files for Windows, ELF files for Linuxs, DMGs for MacOS).
* **Interpreted:** An interpreted language uses an alternative program called the "interpreter" which directly executes your instructions.

Examples of compiled languages include: C, C++, Rust, Go, Zig and more. Python, which is the language I'll be teaching in this course is interpreted. 

I encourage you go ahead and research more about programming languages and how they work. It's a pretty fascinating topic.
