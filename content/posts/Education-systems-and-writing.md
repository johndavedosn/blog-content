To be frank. Most of my work is essentially anti-institutional and no school will have a single clue about how to incorporate it in an educational manner, but if someone manages to do it. I imagine everything I have ever made getting absolutely butchered by teachers and professors.

If anybody is going to explain my stories/books and the meaning conveyed within their texts. It's gonna be me, and I still am against forcing my works in people's faces.

[*I AM THE FIRE* ](https://www.wattpad.com/story/406103950-i-am-the-fire)for instance, is a short story that I wrote through pure inspiration from Lucifer Morningstar and a lot of theology from my general knowledge. The metaphors and themes in it are explored through different layers. (Morals, linguestics, theology, etymology,etc).

Moreover, it delivers multiple messages and each message is independant from the other, and conveyed through different manners and characters. Speaking of characters, I have thought a lot about the general character development of that story, and educational systems might simply not understand many aspects of it.

For example. I'm pretty sure many schools and teachers are going to make Lucifer and Mavet opposites just because of how Lucifer describes Mavet in the first few paragraphs. When in reality, it is nothing but a difference in perspectives which reflects what symbols do they represent.

Mavet is Death - The Destroyer of Worlds. She is described in the story to take genuine pleasure in taking human souls. But there's a reason to that, that pleasure is essentially the only feeling she can ever feel. So naturally, she embraces it. She represents the force of inevitability and the determination of fate.

On the other hand, Lucifer is the iconic pop-culture fallen angel who rebelled against God (or Father, as He was called in IATF). However, in my story, he is much more different and actually a good person who has recognized their mistake and accepted his fate as the eternal Overlord of Hell.

A mistake schools will make (I'm almost sure of this one) is to label Lucifer immediately as Satan - The incarnation of Evil depicted in Abrahamic religions.

While this is an understandable mistake. I can't begin to believe that educational establishments are still to this day unable to distinguish between Lucifer and Satan. It's something that would make an etymology and theology nerd like me go haywire.

Yes. The word "Lucifer" is mentioned in the Bible, but not to describe or name Satan. "Lucifer" is Latin for "light bringer" (Lux = light, Ferre = to bring/to carry), and it is the direct translation by St. Jerome from the Old Hebrew "Helel Ben Shahar", which means "shining one, son of dawn".

The word was initially used to describe Venus - The morning star, in a rather poetic manner as part of a large mockery/allegory of a Babylon king. It was later Medieval Times authors like 
Dante Alighieri (Author of the "Divinia Comedia" - Divine Comedy) that started using "Lucifer" to describe Satan.

But sure. Let's all ignore all of this etymology and pretend that Lucifer is bad just because the label said so. Pathetic. Absolutely pathetic.

Aside from IATF. I have a lot of other works going on. One of which is my own diaries, which I'm planning to publish as a book very later on because they explain a lot about my perspectives on life and such.

Recently. I finally got around to writing a diary entry about a "philosophy" (mind the quotes) I have invented, which I feel like somewhat describes what I follow in my life.

Called "Logicism", it's the philosophy that describes logic as based on self-consistency and coherence with explicit disregard to the basis for said logic. Which means that Logicism accepts any sort of logical framework as valid as long as said framework is consistent to its own rules. The framework can base logic on emotion, religion beliefs, anything.

Logicism is absolutist. Meaning that it rejects all sorts of external validation and relies solely on internal coherence as a source of legitimacy for logical frameworks. It also is non-moral. Meaning that it blatantly refuses to answer any morality questions or do any sort of moral classification. It's a logic-only philosophy.

Now, this all would sound juicy and somewhat sensical to a lot of philosophy lovers and nerds out there. Maybe they won't agree with it completely but that's a non-issue as long as they understand it properly.

But schools? *Oh boy*. I can already sense teachers doing all sorts of wrong things to this philosophy instead of actually explaining it well. Comparing with other philosophies (Philosophy Pokémon Wars!). Completely butchering the non-morality point as "personal belief" or "Contextual" and so on.
