Creative writing is an interesting profession, or hobby, depending on how you approach. You have many ideas and, characters and worlds on one hand, and then you have blank pages on the other, waiting for you to translate your thoughts to them.

Whether you write fantasy, fiction (with all of its subgenres), romance, horror or a combination of all of those and more. Writers have many characteristics that specify their personalities which are derived from their artistic process. I call the combination of traits specifying a certain writer *Writer's Syndrome*.

It is not a bad thing by nature. But a rather collection of traits that characterize artists in general and writing specifically. The artistic process and specifically that of creative writing changes a person's thinking style and their perspectives.

Below is what I believe to be my own *Writer's Syndrome*.
## 1. Perfectionism.

Kind of a big one, though I'm sure many writers would agree. Don't you find yourself sometimes, at 2 AM, researching the details of the military uniforms of some absurd region that existed in the 1500s for your historical fiction story? Yeah, me too.

From my experience at least. Many writers love to make their works as close to perfection as they can be. So realism, writing style, punctuation just to name a few. We tend to love pouring our blood, sweat and tears into a story and then not be able to write for a month after.

Heck, I've just deleted and rewrote a paragraph twice while writing this, and I do not even consider blog posts to be real writing. How absurd is that...

I find it also funny how people would expect writers to praise their own work and refuse any kind of improvements upon it. When in reality, a writer might quite literally think that some part of their story/novel is bad while readers like it. This is the extent of how far *Writer's Syndrome* might go.

In all fairness. I do slightly despise when someone overly edits my work. Especially if they don't understand the tone or voice I'm going for. Not to mention that some of my writing is off-limits for feedback or suggestions for personal reasons.

## 2. Procrastination.

You've got an idea, an amazing one. Maybe inspiration from some TV show/movie/anime or even from a song or whatever. You have the time to write it. You've got the plotlines, characters, everything planned out, outlined, ready.

Yet...you stare at a blank Google Docs page (or whatever writing software you use) unable to even begin your story. Your mind goes null and void the second you try to come up with even a sentence to begin with. Suddenly, playing Minecraft feels fun now.

"I'll come back to this later" You lie to yourself, knowing that you wouldn't come back to it in the next day, or days, or weeks, or months. And so on.

Welcome to the world of Writing-stan, where inspiration is nowhere near enough to force you to actually put your ideas into words. It takes true willpower to sit down and wrestle with 'em words. Especially if your story or plotline has some mundane scenes or buildup arcs you feel bored writing.

I am utterly ashamed of the amount of stories, ideas and worlds I've disregarded because I simply was FREAKING UNABLE to write them into real stories. It is unbelievable, and in hindsight, I should've put more efforts towards making these ideas real.


## 3. Emotional commitment.

I am extremely serious when asking this question so please mind your sense of humor: 

**Am I the only person who imagines his stories as a movie?**

Because it feels pretty silly and insane at the same damn time. Gosh, I cannot begin to list the amount of times I imagined myself as the main character in an enacted movie of my stories and just..drifted in imagination.

Back when I was writing the first chapter of my story *Blades & Blood*. I genuinely felt so bad for the death of my main character's (Michael) dad that I stopped writing that story for a bloody three months. And it took me another month to write his recovery arc because I felt like I was offending him by letting events move on after such a big occassion.

Not to mention. When I got immersed so much in my story IATF that I actually dreamt that I was Mavet when she was taking Nathan's soul in the start of the short story. It was horrible and pretty fun.

I've been called insane by many people for doing that but seventeen years of having a wild imagination taught me that it's simply how my brain works. I've got the ability to imagine entire scenes, plotlines and characters with so many details that it actually hurts.

## 4. Tone locks.

Writers who are bound by worldbuilding to write in specific tones for different projects. Kindly step aside.

Sometimes. I come in contact with a certain tone or way of speaking that captures my attention. And my writing style and general tone locks on that. 

For instance. I've recently been consuming a lot of media featuring British English and specifically the formal kind. Where you sound like an angry Englishman aggressively stirring his tea and judging the world from near the fireplace. Yes, the fireplace.

Naturally, and as you may have notice, endeared one. I picked up on that specific tone, and now whenever I want to sound angry or frustrated. My brain immediately switches to formal British English and I start speaking like whatever you're reading right now.

Mind you that I've always spoke American English since I learnt to speak English in the first place. And now a few seasons of Lucifer Morningstar and some chapters of Pride & Prejudice completely erased that. Lovely.

Now, I tend to to use "bloody" more than "fuck" and "precisely" more than "exactly", and I like it. 

Get me wrong not. Sounding like Mr. Bennet or Darcy isn't exactly a bad thing in my eyes. Saying "Every savage can dance" unironically sounds fun! (I'm looking at you, Darcy sir).

These are merely some tiny quirks of my own artistic profile. You may have your own, and I highly encourage you to go find them. It is the little things like that that build your voice and your personality as a writer.
