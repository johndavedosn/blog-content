**Picture this:**

You're walking down the street, hands in pockets, hoodie on. Simply trying to get to point B without slipping down a puddle (the rain is insane here). A kid, maybe nine or ten years old, points at you and asks his mom "Mommy, look! why is he all white?!".

This, my endeared reader, is a very normal situation for me. I'd usually just smile to the kid and his mom and then proceed to trip as my eyes measure the distance to the sidewalk wrong. Mainly because I had taken off my glasses due to the rain. 

##### (Seriously, why did nobody invent glasses with windshield wipers yet?).

Let's answer the kid's question, shall we? I am all blonde (as I prefer to say) because of a little silly something called Albinism. Congenital absence of melanin in skin and hair. This genetic defect causes my skin to have no sun rays absorption whatsoever. Rendering my skin and hair completely blonde.

People and especially young ones usually stare at me in a rather weird and creepy manner when I am near them. I grew up to learn that it wasn't bad intent but genuine curiousity in their eyes. I've got no problem with that.

My personal favourite is when they assume I'm a foreigner and immediately start speaking to me in a different language, or talk about me in Arabic thinking that I wouldn't understand. The latter kind-of hurts but it's fine.

I've been asked a fair amount of wild questions that make me want to burst out laughing right there, including: 

* "Do your eyes glow in the dark?"
> No, I'm a human, not a cat. Though I'd love being a cat.
* "What kind of hair dye did you use?"
> Umm. Ask God for the brand name. Just don't get smited.
* *Waves five fingers in my face* "Can you tell how much fingers are these?"
> If I couldn't, we'd have many grave problems on our hands.
* "Are you like..an angel?"
> Given that I don't have wings. No.

Albinism comes with many new extensions to your life that you might like or not. Regardless, you're gonna have to live with all of them.


## How people treat me? The Two Traps.

Many people tend to go completely off-course when dealing with me, especially since you don't talk to to an albino everyday, it's a rather rare genetic thing. There two broad ways people tend to see me in: 

1. **Overpowered human**: I do neither know nor understand what went so wrong here. Why did we begin associating disabilities with superpowers? People just keep assuming that I've got some sort of immense intelligence or maybe I shoot lasers from my eyes. Trust me, I wish I had those things more than you do but my life isn't that simple.
2. **Fragile piece of glass**: This one is both sweet and quietly hurting. When people go ahead and simply believe that you're made of paper and that you might die at the most basic scrap or hit. Truth is, I've had my fair of scrapes and bruises in my life and considering that my fingers are what's writing this blog post now, I'm still alive.

The first one may look somewhat brutal but it is much more managable. The second one kind of stings harder.

People often mistake Albinism - and many other disabilities - for something that makes others physically and mentally weaker, which is not only wrong but also an extremely painful mistake that is hard to correct. 

Because when you assume that osmebody is weak, you automatically attempt to offer help (assuming you're a good person, that is :D), and while that's sweet and all. Maybe you should first ask whether that person needs help in the first place.
